# M&A Workflow Mapping (APA, v1)

This is the substantive content that drives the visualization. Each stage
defines:

- **Plain-English description** (beginner)
- **Practitioner notes** (deeper detail for the depth toggle)
- **Key documents** produced or exchanged
- **Mapped CUAD clause categories** (with the exact CUAD question text in
  parentheses where useful)
- **APA-specific gap-fill clauses** (hand-authored — concepts CUAD does not
  cover well)
- **Glossary terms** (used to drive hover tooltips)

> **Disclaimer (to surface in the UI):** This is an educational visualization,
> not legal advice. CUAD is a corpus of general commercial contracts; clauses
> shown here illustrate clause *types* that also appear in M&A deals but are
> not themselves M&A contract excerpts unless explicitly noted.

## Stage 1 — Strategy & Target Identification *(pre-process, dashed)*

**Beginner:** Before a deal exists, the buyer decides what kind of business it
wants to acquire and identifies one or more candidate targets. This is a
business and strategy step, not a legal step — but it shapes everything that
follows.

**Practitioner:** Buyer defines acquisition criteria (industry, size, geography,
synergies, multiples), engages investment bankers or M&A advisors, builds a
target list, runs preliminary outreach. Internal investment-committee approval
to pursue is usually required before contact.

**Key documents:**

- Acquisition strategy memo (internal)
- Target long list and short list
- Teaser / Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) — *prepared by sell-side
  if the target is being marketed*

**Mapped CUAD clauses:** *(none directly applicable — this stage precedes
contract drafting)*

**APA-specific gap-fills:** *(none)*

**Glossary:** *teaser, CIM, synergy, target, acquirer/buyer*

## Stage 2 — Initial Contact & NDA

**Beginner:** Before the buyer can see the target's confidential information,
both sides sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA, also called a Confidentiality
Agreement). This is almost always the first signed document in a deal.

**Practitioner:** NDAs are typically mutual (both parties may share confidential
info). Key negotiated terms: definition of Confidential Information,
permitted-use carve-out (deal evaluation only), term (often 1-3 years), standstill
provisions (preventing the buyer from making a hostile bid for a period),
non-solicit of employees, residuals clause, and return/destruction obligations.
For auction processes, the seller's lawyers prepare a one-sided NDA on the
seller's terms.

**Key documents:**

- Mutual / one-way NDA (Confidentiality Agreement)
- Sometimes: Clean Team Agreement (for sharing competitively sensitive
  information with a limited group)

**Mapped CUAD clauses:**

- *(CUAD does not cover NDAs as a standalone category, but the following
  clause types appear in NDAs and other agreements:)*
- **Non-Solicit of Employees** ("Highlight the parts that discuss any
  restriction on a party's soliciting of employees of counterparties.")

**APA-specific gap-fills:**

- **Standstill clause** — buyer agrees not to acquire shares of the target,
  make a tender offer, or solicit proxies for a defined period without the
  target's consent.
- **Permitted use clause** — confidential information may be used solely for
  evaluating the proposed transaction.

**Glossary:** *NDA, confidentiality agreement, standstill, clean team,
residuals*

## Stage 3 — Letter of Intent (LOI) / Term Sheet

**Beginner:** The LOI (sometimes called a Term Sheet, Heads of Terms, or
Memorandum of Understanding) is a short document that sketches the main
commercial terms of the deal — price, structure, timeline, exclusivity. Most
of the LOI is **non-binding**, but a few sections (exclusivity, confidentiality,
expenses, governing law) are binding.

**Practitioner:** The LOI signals seriousness and locks the seller into
exclusive negotiations for a defined window (typically 30-90 days) so the
buyer can incur the cost of due diligence without fear of being out-bid. Key
terms: indicative purchase price (or range), deal structure (asset vs share
vs merger — *this is where the path branches*), proposed treatment of debt
and cash, employee retention, conditions to closing, exclusivity period,
expense allocation, and access provisions.

**★ Branching point in the diagram:**

- **Asset Purchase (APA)** — buyer acquires specified assets and assumes
  specified liabilities. Used when the buyer wants to cherry-pick what comes
  across, leave behind unwanted liabilities, or get a step-up in tax basis.
  *(This is the path v1 visualizes.)*
- **Share/Stock Purchase (SPA)** — buyer acquires the equity of the target
  entity, taking everything (assets + liabilities) with it. *(Greyed out in
  v1.)*
- **Statutory Merger** — entities legally combine under merger statutes.
  *(Greyed out in v1.)*

**Key documents:**

- Letter of Intent / Term Sheet (signed by both parties)
- Sometimes a separate Exclusivity Agreement

**Mapped CUAD clauses:**

- **Exclusivity** ("Highlight the parts that discuss exclusive dealing
  commitments...")
- **Governing Law** ("Highlight the parts that discuss which state/country's
  law governs the agreement.")
- **Expiration Date** ("Highlight the parts that discuss the date when the
  contract is no longer in effect.")
- **Effective Date** ("Highlight the parts that discuss the date when the
  contract is effective.")

**APA-specific gap-fills:**

- **Indicative purchase price clause** — *"The aggregate purchase price for the
  Assets shall be approximately US$[●], subject to adjustment for [working
  capital / net debt / [other]]."*
- **Deal structure clause** — *"The Transaction shall be structured as a
  purchase by Buyer of substantially all of the assets and the assumption by
  Buyer of certain specified liabilities of Seller."*
- **No-shop / exclusivity (LOI-specific)** — *"Until the earlier of [date] or
  termination of this Letter, Seller shall not solicit, encourage, or enter
  into discussions with any third party regarding any alternative
  transaction."*
- **Non-binding nature clause** — *"Except for [Exclusivity, Confidentiality,
  Expenses, Governing Law], this Letter is non-binding and creates no legal
  obligation."*

**Glossary:** *LOI, term sheet, heads of terms, MOU, exclusivity, no-shop,
indicative price, working capital adjustment, asset deal, share deal, merger*

## Stage 4 — Due Diligence

**Beginner:** The buyer investigates the target in detail to confirm what it
is buying and to find any problems. Lawyers, accountants, and other specialists
review documents the seller places in a virtual data room. The findings shape
the price, the warranties, and sometimes whether the deal happens at all.

**Practitioner:** DD runs in parallel streams — legal, financial, tax,
commercial, IP, employment, environmental, IT, regulatory. Findings feed into:
(i) price adjustments or earn-out negotiations, (ii) specific indemnities for
identified risks, (iii) closing conditions (e.g., third-party consents that
must be obtained), (iv) disclosure schedules (carve-outs from reps and
warranties). For an APA specifically, DD focuses heavily on which assets are
transferable: contracts with anti-assignment clauses require counterparty
consent; IP must be properly assigned; employees must be consulted/transferred
under applicable law.

**Key documents:**

- Due diligence request list
- Virtual data room (VDR) index
- Q&A log
- Legal / financial / tax / commercial DD reports
- Red-flag report (summary of material findings)

**Mapped CUAD clauses (high relevance for APA DD):**

- **Anti-Assignment** ("Highlight the parts that discuss anti-assignment
  provisions...") — *critical for APAs: each material contract must be
  reviewed to see whether counterparty consent is needed to transfer it.*
- **Change of Control** ("Highlight the parts that discuss change of control
  provisions...") — *triggers consent requirements, termination rights, or
  payment obligations under existing contracts.*
- **IP Ownership Assignment** ("Highlight the parts that discuss IP
  assignment provisions...") — *confirms target actually owns its IP, not
  just licenses it.*
- **License Grant** / **Non-Transferable License** — *which IP licenses the
  target relies on, and whether they survive the deal.*
- **Source Code Escrow** — *for software targets, who holds the code and on
  what conditions it's released.*
- **Audit Rights** — *what counterparties can audit the target post-closing.*
- **Most Favored Nation (MFN)** — *price/terms ratchets that may be triggered
  by the deal.*
- **Minimum Commitment** / **Volume Restriction** — *commercial obligations
  the buyer is inheriting.*
- **Affiliate License-Licensor** / **Affiliate License-Licensee** — *whether
  affiliate-of-counterparty rights extend to the buyer post-closing.*

**APA-specific gap-fills:**

- **Material contract review summary** — list of contracts with
  anti-assignment / change-of-control issues requiring consent.
- **Disclosure schedule preparation** — *"Schedule [3.x] sets forth a true and
  complete list of all Material Contracts, indicating those that require
  consent of any third party in connection with the Transaction."*
- **Permits and licenses transferability** — regulatory permits often cannot
  be assigned and must be reissued.
- **Employee diligence** — works council consultations (in some
  jurisdictions), employee benefits transfer, retention agreements.

**Glossary:** *due diligence, data room (VDR), red flags, disclosure schedule,
material contract, consent, transferability, works council*

## Stage 5 — APA Drafting & Negotiation

**Beginner:** With DD findings in hand, the lawyers draft and negotiate the
Asset Purchase Agreement — the main contract. It's typically 60-150 pages and
sets out exactly what's being sold, for how much, with what promises, and what
happens if those promises turn out to be wrong.

**Practitioner:** The APA's main building blocks: (1) Definitions; (2)
Purchase and Sale of Assets (defined Purchased Assets, Excluded Assets,
Assumed Liabilities, Excluded Liabilities); (3) Purchase Price (with
adjustment mechanism — typically locked-box or completion-accounts); (4)
Closing mechanics; (5) Conditions Precedent to Closing; (6) Representations &
Warranties (seller's reps about the business, buyer's reps about itself);
(7) Covenants (interim-period operating covenants, post-closing covenants
including non-compete and non-solicit); (8) Indemnification (limitations of
liability, baskets, caps, survival, sole-and-exclusive remedy); (9)
Termination; (10) General provisions. Disclosure schedules are negotiated in
parallel and can run hundreds of pages.

**Key documents:**

- Asset Purchase Agreement (APA)
- Disclosure Schedules (seller's exceptions to its reps and warranties)
- Form ancillary documents (attached as exhibits): Bill of Sale, Assignment
  and Assumption Agreement, IP Assignment Agreement, Transition Services
  Agreement (TSA), Escrow Agreement
- Financing commitment letters (if buyer is using debt)

**Mapped CUAD clauses (heavily used in APA drafting):**

- **Parties** ("Highlight the parties to the contract.")
- **Agreement Date** / **Effective Date**
- **Cap on Liability** ("Highlight the parts that discuss the maximum
  liability...") — *the indemnification cap.*
- **Uncapped Liability** ("Highlight the parts that discuss any exceptions to
  the cap on liability...") — *typically fraud, intentional misconduct, and
  fundamental reps.*
- **Warranty Duration** ("Highlight the parts that discuss the duration of
  any warranty...") — *the survival period for reps and warranties.*
- **Insurance** — *required insurance coverage, often including R&W
  insurance.*
- **Liquidated Damages** — *break fees in some structures.*
- **Covenant Not To Sue** — *appears in releases ancillary to closing.*
- **Third Party Beneficiary** — *whether non-parties can enforce.*
- **Governing Law** — *choice of law for the APA itself.*

**APA-specific gap-fills (the heart of an APA):**

- **Purchased Assets clause** — *"Subject to the terms and conditions of this
  Agreement, at the Closing, Seller shall sell, transfer, convey, assign and
  deliver to Buyer, and Buyer shall purchase and acquire from Seller, all of
  Seller's right, title and interest in and to the assets set forth on
  Schedule 2.1 (the 'Purchased Assets'), free and clear of all Liens..."*
- **Excluded Assets clause** — explicit list of what is NOT being sold.
- **Assumed Liabilities clause** — explicit list of liabilities buyer takes.
- **Excluded Liabilities clause** — explicit list of liabilities seller keeps
  (typically includes pre-closing tax liabilities, pre-closing litigation,
  product liability for pre-closing sales, environmental liabilities).
- **Purchase Price + adjustment mechanism** — *"The Purchase Price shall be
  US$[●] (the 'Base Purchase Price'), subject to adjustment as follows:
  (a) increased or decreased dollar-for-dollar by the amount by which Closing
  Working Capital exceeds or falls short of the Target Working Capital;
  (b) decreased by Closing Indebtedness; (c) decreased by Transaction
  Expenses..."*
- **Representations and Warranties of Seller** (typical categories):
  organisation; authority; no conflicts; financial statements; absence of
  changes; title to assets; sufficiency of assets; intellectual property;
  contracts; employees; benefits; tax; litigation; compliance with laws;
  permits; environmental; brokers.
- **Material Adverse Change (MAC) clause** — *"From the date hereof until the
  Closing Date, no Material Adverse Effect shall have occurred and be
  continuing."* MAC is one of the most heavily negotiated provisions.
- **Indemnification basket / deductible** — *"Seller shall not be liable for
  Losses unless the aggregate amount of Losses exceeds US$[●] (the
  'Deductible'), in which case Seller shall be liable only for Losses in
  excess of the Deductible."*
- **Indemnification cap** — typically a percentage of purchase price (often
  10-20% for a general cap, 100% for fundamental reps).
- **Survival periods** — general reps survive 12-24 months; tax and
  fundamental reps longer; some indefinite.
- **Specific indemnities** — for risks identified in DD that don't fit a
  general rep.
- **Sandbagging / anti-sandbagging clause** — whether buyer can claim for
  breaches it knew about pre-signing.

**Glossary:** *APA, disclosure schedule, R&W, purchase price adjustment,
locked-box, completion accounts, MAC / MAE, basket, deductible, cap, survival,
fundamental reps, sandbagging, indemnification, escrow, R&W insurance,
TSA (transition services agreement)*

## Stage 6 — Signing

**Beginner:** Both parties execute (sign) the APA and all the agreed ancillary
documents. In most M&A deals, signing and closing are *not* the same day —
there is a gap (often 30-180 days) during which conditions to closing must
be satisfied.

**Practitioner:** Signing is the moment of contractual commitment, even if
ownership doesn't transfer until closing. Disclosure schedules are bringing-up
to-date one final time. Some deals are "simultaneous sign-and-close" if no
regulatory approvals or third-party consents are needed — but most APAs
involve a gap.

**Key documents:**

- Executed APA + Disclosure Schedules
- Executed ancillary documents (or agreed-form exhibits to be executed at
  closing)
- Officer's certificates as to authority
- Press releases (often coordinated with signing announcement)

**Mapped CUAD clauses:**

- **Agreement Date** / **Effective Date**
- **Parties**

**APA-specific gap-fills:**

- **Execution clause** — *"IN WITNESS WHEREOF, the parties hereto have caused
  this Agreement to be executed as of the date first written above."*

**Glossary:** *signing, execution, simultaneous sign-and-close, sign-to-close
gap*

## Stage 7 — Pre-Closing / Conditions Satisfaction

**Beginner:** Between signing and closing, the parties work to satisfy the
closing conditions: getting regulatory approvals, getting third-party consents,
arranging financing, and confirming nothing has gone badly wrong with the
business.

**Practitioner:** Typical closing conditions: (i) regulatory clearances
(antitrust filings such as HSR in the US, merger control in EU/UK/other
jurisdictions, FDI/foreign investment screening, sectoral approvals); (ii)
material third-party consents (assignment of key contracts, change-of-control
consents under loans/leases/IP licenses); (iii) no MAC; (iv) reps remaining
true at closing (bring-down certificate); (v) interim-period covenants
complied with; (vi) financing in place; (vii) no injunction. Interim covenants
restrict how seller can run the business between sign and close (must operate
in the ordinary course; cannot incur new debt, sell assets, hire/fire
above thresholds, etc.).

**Key documents:**

- Antitrust / regulatory filings (HSR Form, Form CO, etc.)
- Third-party consent letters (executed copies as they come in)
- Bring-down certificates
- Officer's certificates re: condition satisfaction
- Disclosure schedule updates (if permitted)

**Mapped CUAD clauses:**

- **Anti-Assignment** — *consents being chased to satisfy this stage are
  driven by anti-assignment provisions identified in DD.*
- **Change of Control** — *consents and notices triggered by these clauses
  must be addressed before closing.*

**APA-specific gap-fills:**

- **Conditions Precedent clause** — *"The obligations of Buyer to consummate
  the Closing shall be subject to the satisfaction (or waiver by Buyer) of
  the following conditions: (a) the representations and warranties of Seller
  shall be true and correct in all material respects as of the Closing Date;
  (b) Seller shall have performed in all material respects all covenants
  required to be performed by it; (c) all required Consents shall have been
  obtained; (d) all required Regulatory Approvals shall have been obtained;
  (e) no Material Adverse Effect shall have occurred; (f) Buyer shall have
  received a certificate of an officer of Seller certifying as to the
  foregoing..."*
- **Interim operating covenants** — *"Between the date hereof and the
  Closing, Seller shall conduct the Business only in the ordinary course
  consistent with past practice, and shall not, without the prior written
  consent of Buyer: (i) sell or transfer any Purchased Asset other than in
  the ordinary course; (ii) incur any Indebtedness; (iii) increase
  compensation of any employee above [●]; (iv) enter into any Material
  Contract..."*
- **Reasonable best efforts / hell-or-high-water clause** — how hard buyer
  must push to get antitrust clearance (heavily negotiated).
- **Termination right for failure to close** — typically a "drop-dead date"
  (long-stop date / outside date).

**Glossary:** *closing conditions, conditions precedent, HSR, antitrust
clearance, FDI screening, third-party consent, bring-down certificate,
interim covenants, ordinary course, drop-dead date, long-stop date, hell-or-
high-water*

## Stage 8 — Closing / Completion

**Beginner:** The day the deal actually happens. Money flows from buyer to
seller, and ownership of the assets transfers from seller to buyer. A
choreographed sequence of document deliveries and wire transfers takes place
— often coordinated by lawyers via a closing checklist.

**Practitioner:** Closing is mechanical but high-stakes. A pre-closing
"closing memorandum" lists every document and action in order. On the day:
(i) condition satisfaction confirmed; (ii) ancillary documents executed;
(iii) bills of sale, assignments, IP assignments delivered; (iv) employee
transfer effected; (v) escrow funded; (vi) purchase price wired (less escrow,
working capital estimate, transaction expenses); (vii) closing certificates
exchanged; (viii) keys handed over (literally for real estate; figuratively
for systems access). Post-closing wire confirmation ends the day.

**Key documents:**

- Closing memorandum / closing checklist
- Bill of Sale
- Assignment and Assumption Agreement
- IP Assignment Agreement (separate document for trademarks, patents,
  copyrights, often with country-specific forms)
- Real Estate transfer documents (deeds, leases assignments)
- Escrow Agreement (funded at closing)
- Transition Services Agreement (effective at closing)
- Funds flow memorandum
- Closing certificates (officer's, secretary's, good-standing)
- Resignation letters (officers/directors of acquired entities)

**Mapped CUAD clauses:**

- **Effective Date** — *the closing date is typically the effective date for
  many ancillary agreements.*
- **IP Ownership Assignment** — *the IP Assignment Agreement is signed and
  delivered at closing.*
- **License Grant** — *if seller is licensing back any retained IP, that
  license is granted at closing.*

**APA-specific gap-fills:**

- **Bill of Sale** — *"Seller hereby sells, transfers, conveys, assigns and
  delivers to Buyer, and Buyer hereby accepts, all of Seller's right, title
  and interest in and to the Purchased Assets..."*
- **Funds flow** — line-item allocation of the wire: to seller, to escrow,
  to repay debt, to pay transaction expenses, to employees (retention/bonus).

**Glossary:** *closing, completion, bill of sale, assignment and assumption,
funds flow, escrow, closing certificate, secretary's certificate, good
standing*

## Stage 9 — Post-Closing

**Beginner:** The deal is "done" but a lot of work remains. The purchase
price gets finalised after closing accounts are agreed. The buyer integrates
the business. Indemnity claims may surface. The seller is bound by non-
compete and non-solicit promises. Escrow funds are released over time.

**Practitioner:** Major workstreams: (1) Purchase price true-up — closing
working capital is finalised within 60-120 days, dispute mechanism if parties
disagree; (2) Indemnification claims — buyer makes claims as breaches surface,
subject to baskets, caps, survival, and notice procedures; (3) Escrow release
— typically tranche releases at, e.g., 12 and 18 months, less held-back
amounts for pending claims; (4) Earn-out measurement — for deals with
contingent consideration, the earn-out period (1-3 years typical) involves
defined metrics, dispute mechanism, and conduct-of-business covenants;
(5) Non-compete / non-solicit enforcement — typically 2-5 year restrictive
covenants on seller and key principals; (6) Transition services — buyer
relies on seller for IT/HR/finance for 3-12 months under the TSA;
(7) Integration — separate from legal, but legal supports.

**Key documents:**

- Closing accounts / completion accounts (and dispute resolution)
- Indemnification claim notices
- Escrow release instructions
- Earn-out statements
- TSA exit notices
- Tax filings (Section 338(h)(10) election in US; transfer tax filings; VAT)

**Mapped CUAD clauses:**

- **Non-Compete** ("Highlight the parts that discuss restrictions on a
  party from competing...") — *seller and key sellers commit not to compete
  in the same business for a defined period and territory.*
- **No-Solicit of Customers** — *seller agrees not to solicit transferred
  customers.*
- **No-Solicit of Employees** — *seller agrees not to solicit transferred
  employees.*
- **Non-Disparagement** — *parties agree not to disparage each other.*
- **Post-Termination Services** — *appears in TSAs governing the wind-down
  period.*
- **Warranty Duration** — *governs how long indemnity claims can be brought.*
- **Cap on Liability** / **Uncapped Liability** — *limits and exceptions to
  indemnification.*
- **Competitive Restriction Exception** — *carve-outs to non-compete (e.g.,
  passive investments under [5%], pre-existing minority interests).*

**APA-specific gap-fills:**

- **Closing accounts dispute mechanism** — *"If Buyer and Seller cannot agree
  on the Closing Statement within [30] days, the dispute shall be referred
  to [an Independent Accountant] whose determination shall be final and
  binding."*
- **Indemnification claim procedure** — *"Buyer shall give Seller written
  notice of any claim within [30] days of becoming aware of it. Seller shall
  have [30] days to respond. If disputed, the parties shall negotiate in
  good faith for [30] days before commencing proceedings."*
- **Escrow release schedule** — *"The Escrow Amount shall be released as
  follows: 50% on the [12-month] anniversary of Closing; the balance on the
  [18-month] anniversary, in each case less amounts held back for pending
  claims."*
- **Earn-out clause** (where applicable) — *"Buyer shall pay Seller an
  Earn-Out Payment of up to US$[●] based on the Business's EBITDA for the
  [12-month] period ending [date], calculated as follows..."*
- **Conduct of business during earn-out** — protective covenants for the
  seller to ensure earn-out is achievable.

**Glossary:** *true-up, closing accounts, completion accounts, indemnification,
basket, cap, survival, escrow release, earn-out, EBITDA, non-compete,
non-solicit, restrictive covenant, TSA exit, integration*

## Cross-cutting: how the Beginner ↔ Practitioner toggle works

For each stage, the side panel renders:

- **Beginner mode (default):** the "Beginner" paragraph + key documents +
  glossary terms surfaced as inline tooltips.
- **Practitioner mode:** Beginner content + Practitioner paragraph + full list
  of mapped CUAD clauses (with quoted excerpts) + APA-specific gap-fill
  clauses (with example wording).

The toggle is a single state variable at the page level; the glossary tooltips
are always available regardless of mode.

## Cross-cutting: clause provenance UI

Every CUAD clause excerpt shown in the UI must include:

- The CUAD category name (e.g., "Anti-Assignment")
- The original CUAD question text (so users see what the lawyer-annotators
  were asked)
- The contract title from CUAD (e.g., "Acme License Agreement, 2018")
- A "from CUAD dataset" pill linking to the HF dataset page

Hand-authored APA gap-fill clauses must be marked with a distinct visual
treatment (e.g., italic + "illustrative example" pill) so users do not
mistake them for real contract excerpts.
